What is Drifting?

Drifting is a driving technique where the driver intentionally oversteers, causing a loss of traction in the rear wheels while maintaining control from entry to exit of a corner. Originating in Japan, drifting has evolved into an internationally recognized motorsport that tests a driver's skill and precision.

The History of Drifting

Drifting began in the mountainous roads of Japan during the 1970s, pioneered by drivers who would race down the winding slopes. These early street racers perfected the art of cornering with finesse, leading to the popularization of the technique. Eventually, professional events began emerging, with the first official drift event taking place in Japan in 1988. Today, drifting has a global following, with major competitions in the United States, Europe, and Asia.

Types of Drifting Events

Modern drifting events come in various formats, from grassroots gatherings to internationally sanctioned competitions. Some of the most popular events include:

  • Professional Competitions: These events, like Formula Drift and D1 Grand Prix, bring together top drivers from around the world to compete in high-stakes rounds. Judging criteria often include speed, angle, line, and style.
  • Grassroots Events: These are more accessible to amateurs and enthusiasts who want to experience the thrill of drifting in a less formal setting. Grassroots events offer a relaxed environment to practice and learn.
  • Exhibition Shows: Drifting exhibitions are usually held as part of car shows or auto expos, where skilled drivers perform stunts and showcase their control to entertain crowds.

When to Drift

The best times to drift are during organized events, often held on weekends or during specific seasons. Here's a guide:

  • Spring & Summer: Perfect for outdoor drifting due to dry weather and warm temperatures.
  • Fall: Cooler temperatures make it easier on the engines and tires, plus beautiful seasonal backdrops.
  • Winter: Challenging for seasoned drivers who want to test their skills on slippery surfaces.

Most events occur on weekends, but private practice sessions can be scheduled year-round at certain tracks.

How to Drift

If you're interested in exploring the world of drifting, here are some tips to get started:

  1. Research: Familiarize yourself with drifting techniques and terminology.
  2. Join a Community: Connect with local drifting clubs and attend events.
  3. Practice: Find a safe and controlled environment to practice your drifting skills.
  4. Invest in a Drift Car: Consider a car that is popular in the drifting community.
  5. Take Lessons: Consider taking professional drifting lessons for guidance.

Steps to Drift

Once you're ready to try drifting, follow these steps:

  1. Find the Right Location: Look for a wide, open space with a smooth surface, such as an empty parking lot or a track.
  2. Warm Up Your Tires: Drive your car in circles to heat up the tires, which helps improve grip when you begin drifting.
  3. Approach the Turn: As you approach a corner, gain speed (ideally between 30-50 mph) while positioning your car toward the outside of the turn.
  4. Initiate the Drift: Use the "clutch kick" method: press the clutch pedal, rev the engine, and quickly release the clutch to break traction.
  5. Steer into the Drift: Turn the steering wheel in the direction of the turn while modulating the throttle to maintain control and angle.
  6. Countersteer: As the back of the car begins to slide out, countersteer (turn the wheel in the opposite direction) to regain control.
  7. Throttle Control: Adjust the throttle as needed to maintain the drift. Too much throttle can cause you to spin out, while too little can let the grip return.
  8. Exit the Drift: As you complete the turn, straighten the steering wheel and gradually ease off the throttle to regain full traction.
